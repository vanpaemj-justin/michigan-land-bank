Why Ottawa County?

Ottawa County is West Michigan's growth engine. Situated between Grand Rapids and Lake Michigan, it includes cities like Holland, Hudsonville, and Zeeland. With a strong economy, growing population, and constrained developable land, it's a prime market for land investors.

Key Zoning & Land Use

Residential (R1, R2, R3)

  • Best for: Spec homes, subdivisions, rental properties
  • Typical lot size: 0.25 – 1.5 acres
  • Permits required: Building permit, well/septic if not on city utilities
  • Avg. price range: $45K – $250K per acre

Agricultural (AG)

  • Best for: Hobby farms, solar farms, long‑term holds
  • Typical lot size: 5 – 40 acres
  • Permits required: Minimal for agricultural use
  • Avg. price range: $6K – $12K per acre

Commercial / Industrial

  • Best for: Retail pads, storage units, light industrial
  • Typical lot size: 1 – 5 acres
  • Permits required: Site plan review, zoning variance
  • Avg. price range: $125K – $400K per acre

Where to Find Deals

  1. Ottawa County Tax Auction — Held annually in September. Check ottawacounty.com/treasurer for dates and listings.
  2. MLS — Work with a local realtor familiar with Ottawa County. Search for vacant land, filter by city.
  3. Direct Mail — Target property owners with delinquent taxes or those in agricultural zoning who may be open to selling.
  4. OnX Hunt — Essential for parcel research, boundaries, and ownership data.

Due Diligence Checklist

  • ✓ Title search (~$150 via local title company)
  • ✓ Zoning verification (Ottawa County Planning: 616-738-4000)
  • ✓ Perc test results — required for any septic system
  • ✓ Flood zone determination (FEMA maps)
  • ✓ Utility access — check with Consumers Energy and local municipal water/sewer
  • ✓ Survey (recommended, ~$500-$1,000)

Why Investors Are Watching Ottawa County

  • Lake Michigan proximity — Demand driven by tourism and seasonal housing
  • Growing job market — Presence of Gentex, Haworth, and other major employers fuels housing demand
  • Spatial constraints — Limited developable land due to wetlands and agricultural preservation zones
  • Infrastructure investment — New roads and utilities opening previously hard‑to‑develop areas

Risks to Watch

  • Wetland issues — Significant protected wetlands in Holland and Zeeland areas; always check USDA Web Soil Survey
  • Zoning complexity — Some townships have strict agricultural preservation zoning
  • Seasonal demand — Lake‑adjacent properties see price spikes in spring/summer
