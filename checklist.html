5 Steps to Buying Your First Michigan Land

Here's the exact system I use to find profitable Michigan land deals — free from Michigan Land Bank.

✅ Step 1: How to Find Off‑Market Deals

  • County Tax Auctions — Sign up for alerts from every county treasurer in your target area. Auctions happen year‑round, but the big annual sales are March‑April (most counties).
  • Direct Mail Campaigns — Mail letters to property owners with delinquent tax bills. Use a skip‑tracing service to find addresses. Your offer: "We buy land as‑is — no fees."
  • MLS (Multiple Listing Service) — Work with a local realtor who specializes in land. Set up automated alerts for "vacant land" in your target counties.
  • OnX Hunt / Landgrid — Use parcel maps to find owners of 5+ acre parcels and contact them directly.

✅ Step 2: County Zoning Research Shortcuts

  • Call the County Planning & Zoning Office — Ask for the zoning district and permitted uses.
  • Check wetland maps (USDA Web Soil Survey) — Wetlands = no buildable area without expensive mitigation.
  • Verify road frontage — Must have at least 33 ft on a public road, or a recorded easement.
  • Ask about setback requirements — Setbacks reduce your buildable area significantly.

✅ Step 3: Auction vs. Private Sale Math

  • Auction Price = Winning bid + Buyer's premium (usually 10‑20%) + Transfer taxes
  • Private Sale Price = Asking price + Closing costs (typically 5‑10%)
  • Rule of thumb — If auction price > 70% of ARV (after repair value), walk away.
  • Carry costs — Budget for 12‑24 months of property taxes, insurance, and loan payments.

✅ Step 4: Title Insurance Red Flags

  • Always get a title search (~$150) — Don't skip this!
  • Surviving mortgages — If there's a mortgage, it survives foreclosure. You're on the hook.
  • Tax liens — Usually wiped out at auction, but verify with the title company.
  • Easements — Check for utility easements or right‑of‑way that limit your use.
  • Quiet title action — If title is cloudy, budget $1,500‑$3,000 for a lawyer to clear it.

✅ Step 5: Exit Strategy Options

  • Sell to a developer — Hold for 3‑5 years, sell to a builder when area gentrifies.
  • Lease for hunting/recreation — $500‑$2,000/year for hunting leases.
  • Seller financing — Land contract to a buyer with 20% down, 8‑10% interest.
  • BRRRR (Buy, Rent, Rent, Refinance, Repeat) — Not as applicable for land, but consider lease‑to‑own structures.

Bonus: My Favorite Tools

  • USDA Web Soil Survey — Free wetland/drainage info
  • County Treasurer Websites — Free auction listings
  • SkipTrace Pro — $30/month for owner contact info
