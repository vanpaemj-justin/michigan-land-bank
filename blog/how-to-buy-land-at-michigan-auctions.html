How to Buy Land at Michigan County Auctions

A step-by-step guide to finding, bidding, and closing on discounted land

Home → Blog → How to Buy Land at Michigan Auctions

April 6, 2026 • 12 min read

Michigan county tax auctions are one of the best ways to find discounted land — but they're also where investors lose money if they don't do their homework. I've bought dozens of parcels at auction over the years, and in this guide I'll walk you through the entire process.

What Is a County Tax Auction?

When property owners fail to pay property taxes, the county forecloses on the lien. The county then sells the property at a public auction to recover the owed taxes. These sales can offer significant discounts — often 30-70% below market value.

Step 1: Find Upcoming Auctions

Each county holds its own auction, typically once per year. Here's how to find them:

  • County Treasurer Websites — Search "[County Name] Michigan tax auction" or visit the treasurer's page. Most post auction dates 30-60 days in advance.
  • Google Alerts — Set up alerts for "tax auction Michigan" and your target counties.
  • Tax Sale List — Some counties publish a printable list of all parcels. Request one if available.

Step 2: Research the Parcels

Never bid on a property without researching it first. Here's your due diligence checklist:

  • Current Owner — Who owns it now? Is there a mortgage or lien that survives the sale?
  • Title Status — Demand a title search (~$150). Some liens (like mortgages) survive foreclosure and become your problem.
  • Zoning — Check with the county planning department. Is it buildable? Agricultural? Restricted?
  • Access — Does the parcel have road frontage? If not, you may need an easement.
  • Utilities — Are water/sewer available? If not, what's the cost to connect?
  • Environmental — Check for wetlands, contamination, or other issues.

Pro Tip: The "Drive-By" Test

Before bidding, physically visit the property if possible. I've shown up to parcels that were completely underwater, inaccessible, or had a house on them despite being listed as "vacant land."

Step 3: Determine Your Max Bid

Calculate the maximum you'll pay based on:

  1. After-Repair Value (ARV) — What's the land worth after you fix it/sell it?
  2. Repair Costs — Clearing, grading, permit fees, utilities
  3. Carrying Costs — Property taxes, insurance, loan payments during hold
  4. Your Profit Margin — At least 20-30% for risk and effort

Never bid emotionally — stick to your numbers.

Step 4: Register and Bid

  • Registration — Most auctions require现场 registration with ID and a deposit (usually $1,000-$5,000).
  • Bidding Methods — Some are live, others are online (Bid4Assets, GovAuctions). Know the format beforehand.
  • Winning — If you win, you'll need to pay immediately or within 24 hours. Bring a cashier's check.

Step 5: Close and Take Possession

  • Pay in Full — Auctions are typically "cash or check" — no financing contingencies.
  • Get a Deed — After payment, you'll receive a tax deed. File it at the county register of deeds.
  • Quiet Period — Some states have a redemption period where the owner can pay off back taxes and reclaim the property. In Michigan, this varies by county — know the rules.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the title search — A surviving mortgage can cost you thousands.
  • Not visiting the property — You don't know what you're buying until you see it.
  • Overbidding in the heat of the moment — Set a max bid and stick to it.
  • Ignoring zoning — Always verify permitted uses before bidding.

Final Thoughts

County auctions can be incredibly profitable — I've purchased land for $5,000 that was worth $25,000. But the risk is real. Do your homework, stick to your numbers, and never spend more than you can afford to lose.

Start with your target county, research a few parcels, and attend an auction (even just to watch) before you bid.
