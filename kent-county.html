Why Kent County?

Kent County is the economic engine of West Michigan, anchored by Grand Rapids — the state's second-largest city. With a growing population, strong demand for housing, and limited developable land, the county offers both immediate rental potential and long-term appreciation.

Key Zoning & Land Use

Most vacant land in Kent County falls into a few categories:

Residential (R1, R2, R3)

  • Best for: Building spec homes, subdivisions, rental properties
  • Typical lot size: 0.25 – 2 acres
  • Permits required: Building permit, well/septic if not on city utilities
  • Avg. price range: $50K – $300K per acre (depending on location)

Agricultural (AG)

  • Best for: Hobby farms, solar farms, investment holds
  • Typical lot size: 5 – 40 acres
  • Permits required: Minimal for agricultural use
  • Avg. price range: $5K – $15K per acre

Commercial / Industrial

  • Best for: Long-term development plays, storage units, retail pads
  • Typical lot size: 1 – 10 acres
  • Permits required: Site plan review, zoning variance often needed
  • Avg. price range: $150K – $500K per acre

Where to Find Deals

  1. Kent County Tax Auction – Held annually in March. Best source for discounted distressed properties. Start at kentcountytreasurer.gov.
  2. MLS (Multiple Listing Service) – Work with a local realtor. Search for "vacant land" filtered by Kent County.
  3. Direct Mail – Send letters to property owners with outstanding tax bills. I use a skip-tracing service to get addresses.
  4. OnX Hunt (formerly OnX) – Great for finding parcel ownership and boundary info. Essential for due diligence.

Due Diligence Checklist

  • ✓ Title search (via local title company, ~$150)
  • ✓ Zoning verification (Kent County Planning & Development: 616-632-3880)
  • ✓ Perc test results (if planning septic) – required for building permits
  • ✓ Flood zone determination (FEMA Flood Map Service Center)
  • ✓ Utility access verification (Consumers Energy for gas/electric, municipal for water/sewer)
  • ✓ Survey (recommended, ~$500-$1,500 depending on size)

Risks to Watch

  • High competition — Expect to bid 10-20% above asking at auction
  • Zoning delays – Permits can take 8-12 weeks; plan accordingly
  • Wetland issues – Use the USDA Web Soil Survey to check hydric soils before buying
